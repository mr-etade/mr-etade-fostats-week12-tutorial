Residuals & Causation

A high $R^2$ doesn't guarantee the model is right, or that the relationship is causal. Two essential diagnostics: residual plots, and a hard look for lurking variables.

Three residual plot patterns

Toggle between the patterns. Each tells you something different about whether the linear model is appropriate.

Verdict: Random scatter ⇒ linear model is appropriate

Why this matters: $R^2$ alone can be high even when the model is wrong. The residual plot is what catches it. If you see a clear pattern, the line is missing something — either curvature or non-constant variance.

Questions

Q1 1 mark

An IT student fits a linear regression of server CPU temperature ($y$, °C) against time of day ($x$, hours since midnight) at a Madang ISP. The residual plot shows a clear U-shape: residuals are positive at the beginning and end of the day, negative in the middle.

What should the student do?

Q2 2 marks

A cybersecurity analyst at a Port Moresby bank notices that the daily number of failed login attempts ($x$) and the daily number of customer support tickets ($y$) are strongly positively correlated, with $r = 0.89$.

Which is the most likely explanation, and why is "failed logins cause more support tickets" the wrong conclusion to draw from $r$ alone?

Q3 2 marks

A WPU network engineer fits a regression of server response time ($y$, ms) against number of concurrent users ($x$). The fitted line has $R^2 = 0.91$, but the residual plot shows that the spread of residuals is small for low values of $x$ and grows much wider as $x$ increases.

Which best describes this pattern, and what is the practical implication?
