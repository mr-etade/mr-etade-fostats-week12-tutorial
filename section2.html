$R^2$ — Explained Variation

$R^2 = r^2$ is the proportion of variation in $y$ that the line explains. Drag the slope and intercept and watch the green (explained) and red (residual) segments swap.

Live explorer

The yellow points are fixed data. Adjust the slope and intercept to fit a line. Green segments show what the line explains; red segments show what's left over (residuals).

Try this: set the slope to $0$ (a horizontal line at $c = \bar{y}$). All variation becomes residual — $R^2 \to 0$. That's the worst-case "no model" baseline.
Try this: click "Snap to best-fit line". $R^2$ jumps to its maximum — the green segments dominate the red. This is what least squares gives you.

A regression of monthly cloud-storage cost ($y$, in PGK) against number of active users ($x$) at a Port Moresby SaaS startup gives Pearson's $r = -0.82$. Compute the coefficient of determination $R^2$, rounded to 3 decimal places.

A study fits a linear regression of the number of defects found in code review ($y$) against minutes of review time ($x$) for $n = 40$ pull requests at a Lae fintech company. The result is $R^2 = 0.684$.

Which interpretation is correct?
